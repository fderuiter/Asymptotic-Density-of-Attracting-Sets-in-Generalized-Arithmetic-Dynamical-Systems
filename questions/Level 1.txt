### 1. The Structural Prerequisite: Identifying Amenable Systems

**Research Question:** *What specific algebraic and structural properties must an integer-valued quasi-polynomial, defined on residue classes modulo $d$, possess to preclude Turing-complete unpredictability and render its arithmetic dynamics amenable to analytic density frameworks?*

* **Why this works (The Strategy):** This is your defensive perimeter. You are explicitly acknowledging the danger of the generalization. By answering this, you establish a rigorous "filtering mechanism" that guarantees you only spend your time analyzing systems that are mathematically possible to solve. You are looking for specific transition matrices or geometric constraints that enforce regularity.

### 2. The Analytic Translation: Adapting the Machinery

**Research Question:** *How can existing analytic counting frameworks—specifically the difference inequalities developed by Krasikov and Lagarias, or the logarithmic density techniques introduced by Tao—be rigorously adapted to evaluate the convergence sets of structurally constrained generalized arithmetic maps?*

* **Why this works (The Strategy):** This is the core methodology of your thesis. You aren't inventing a new way to count; you are doing the hard work of translating existing, highly respected analytic number theory tools into a new algebraic environment. This question proves you understand the deep mechanics of how these density theorems actually function under the hood.

### 3. The Core Target: Establishing the Asymptotic Bound

**Research Question:** *For a formally defined and structurally constrained class of quasi-polynomial maps, can it be proven that the counting function $\pi_{\mathcal{A}}(x)$—representing the number of integers strictly less than $x$ converging to an attracting set $\mathcal{A}$—is bounded below by an asymptotic threshold such as $c(\epsilon)x^{1-\epsilon}$ or a corresponding strict logarithmic density?*

* **Why this works (The Strategy):** This is your primary deliverable—the main theorem of your dissertation. It provides the "layperson-friendly" conclusion (the physical counting of numbers dropping into the trap) but executes it within the safe, generalized framework you built in the first two questions. It gives you a specific, numerical target to hit.

### 4. The Grand Synthesis: The Algebraic-Analytic Correspondence

**Research Question:** *Is there a definitive, mathematically formal correspondence between the foundational algebraic parameters of a quasi-polynomial system (e.g., the coefficients $a_i, b_i$ and the modulus $d$) and the necessary conditions for its attracting sets to achieve a strictly positive natural or logarithmic density?*

* **Why this works (The Strategy):** This elevates the thesis from a single, isolated proof into a broader, generalized theory. Instead of just saying "Map X has a high density," this question asks, "What is the universal rule that dictates *why* Map X has a high density while Map Y does not?" It bridges the gap between the algebraists and the analysts on your committee.
